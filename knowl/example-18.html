Example 2.58. Diagonalizing A.

Consider the matrix transformation given by \(A = \left[ \begin{matrix} 3 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 2 \\ \end{matrix} \right].\) As we saw in ExampleÂ 2.56, \(A\) has an eigen-basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) Moreover, we found a transformation from the standard basis to those eigenvectors is given by
\begin{equation*} E =\left[ \begin{matrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 2 \\ \end{matrix} \right]. \end{equation*}
If we want to instead scale an eigenbasis we can first transform our eigen-basis vectors to the standard basis vectors, use a dilation matrix then transform back to our eigenbasis as follows.
  1. Step 1: Find the matrix transformation from the standard basis to the eigenbasis.
    \begin{equation*} E =\left[ \begin{matrix} -1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 1 \\ \end{matrix} \right] \end{equation*}
  2. Step 2: Use the inverse \(E^{-1}\) to transform from the eigenbasis to the standard basis.
  3. Step 3: Find the corresponding dilation matrix
    Since we mapped the eigenvector with eigenvalue of 1 to \(\vec{e}_1\) and the eigenvector with eigenvalue of 4 to \(\vec{e}_2\text{,}\) we want to scale \(\vec{e}_1\) by the eigenvalue of 1 and \(\vec{e}_2\) by the eigenvalue of 4.
    \begin{equation*} D =\left[ \begin{matrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 4 \\ \end{matrix} \right] \end{equation*}
